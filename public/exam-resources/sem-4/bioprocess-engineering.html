Why do Mathematical Modelling?
вҖў Mathematical modeling has always been an important activity in 
science and engineering.
вҖў The formulation of qualitative questions about an observed 
phenomenon as mathematical problems was the motivation for and an 
integral part of the development of mathematics from the very 
beginning.
вҖў Computational power is particularly important in modeling chemical 
engineering systems, as the physical and chemical laws governing these 
processes are complex. 
вҖў Besides heat, mass, and momentum transfer, these processes may also 
include chemical reactions, reaction heat, adsorption, desorption, phase 
transition, multiphase flow, etc. 
вҖў This makes modeling challenging but also necessary to understand 
complex interactions.

[Page 7]
Why do Mathematical Modelling?
вҖў In a chemical engineering context, mathematical modeling is a prerequisite for:
вҖ“ Design and scale-up;
вҖ“ Process control;
вҖ“ Optimization;
вҖ“ Mechanistic understanding;
вҖ“ Evaluation/planning of experiments;
вҖ“ Trouble shooting and diagnostics;
вҖ“ Determining quantities that cannot be measured directly;
вҖ“ Simulation instead of costly experiments in the development lab;
вҖ“ Feasibility studies to determine potential before building prototype equipment or 
devices.

[Page 9]
вҖў Step 1: Problem definition: 
вҖ“ This involves stating clear goals for the modeling, including the various elements that pertain to the 
problem and its solution. (what hypothesis and which assumptions to use)
вҖў Step 2: Formulation of conceptual model: 
вҖ“ Collect data and experience about the subject to be modeled. 
вҖ“ The main challenges are in identifying the underlying mechanisms and governing physical/chemical 
principles of the problem. 
вҖў Step 3: Formulation of mathematical model:
вҖ“ Each important quantity is represented by a suitable mathematical entity, e.g. a variable, a function, a graph, 
etc.
вҖ“ What are the variables (dependent, independent, parameters)?
вҖ“ What are the constraints?
вҖ“ What boundary conditions?
вҖ“ What initial conditions?
вҖў Step 4: Solution of the mathematical problem:
вҖ“ Check the validity of individual mathematical relationships, and whether the relationships are mutually 
consistent. 
вҖ“ V erify the mathematical solution, i.e. ensure that you have solved the equations correctly. This step involves 
checking your solution against previously known results (analytical/numerical), simplified limiting cases, 
etc.

[Page 10]
вҖў Step 5: Estimation of parameters:
вҖ“ The parameters of the system must be evaluated and the appropriate values must be used in the model. Some
parameters can be obtained independently of the mathematical model. (e.g. gravitation constant, solubility data)
вҖ“ However, it is usually not possible to evaluate all the parameters from specific experiments, and many of them
have to be estimated by taking results from the whole (or a similar system), and then using parameter-fitting
techniques to determine which set of parameter values makes the model best fit the experimental results.
вҖ“ For example, a complex reaction may involve ten or more kinetic constants.
вҖ“ These constants can be estimated by fitting a model to results from a laboratory reactor.
вҖў Step 6: Evaluation/validation :
вҖ“ A key step in mathematical modeling is experimental validation.
вҖ“ Ideally the validation should be made using independent experimental results, i.e. not the same set as used for
parameter estimation.
вҖ“ During the validation procedure it may happen that the model still has some deficiencies.
вҖ“ In that case, we have to вҖңiterateвҖқ the model and eventually modify it.
вҖ“ Certain characteristics that models which have a bearing on the question of how good they are: Accuracy;
Descriptive realism; Precision; Robustness; Generality; Fruitfulness.
вҖў Step 7: Interpretation/application:
вҖ“ The validated model is then ready to be used for one or several purposes as described earlier, e.g. to enhance our 
understanding, make predictions, and give information about how to control the process.

Models of growth associated and non growth associated product 
formation
Growth growth associated product formation
вҖў End products of energy and carbon metabolism are primary metabolism
вҖў Examples; ethanol production by anaerobic fermentation glucose by yeast
вҖў Production of gluconic acid from glucose by gluconobactor
вҖў These products are referred to as growth associated products as their rate of 
production parallels the growth of the cell population
Non growth associated product formation
вҖў Products such as vitamins and antibiotics are produced in a batch culture at the end 
of exponential phase
вҖў Such secondary metabolites are termed as Non growth associated products
вҖў Their kinetics does not depend on the rate of growth of the culture

[Page 84]
Partially growth associated product formation
вҖў Product formation lies between the growth associated product formation and Non 
growth associated product formation
вҖў Examples: Amino acids, lactic acid, intermediates from citric acid cycle (citric acid), 
extracellular polysaccharides (Xanthan and pullulan ) and solvents such as acetone 
and butanol

вҖў Penicillin production is subjected to catabolite repression
вҖў A slow supply of carbon source is necessary to ensure that intermediate metabolites 
do not accumulate and reduce production by end product inhibition
